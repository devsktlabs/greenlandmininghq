Greenland Mining History: From Early Exploration to Skaergaard

By Compiled by Editorial Team · 2026-03-23

greenlandhistoryminingnalunaq

Greenland's mining history stretches back centuries, from indigenous use of minerals to modern industrial exploration. Understanding this history provides context for the current mining development opportunity.

Indigenous Use of Minerals

Greenlandic Inuit have used minerals for thousands of years:

  • Soapstone (steatite): Carved into cooking pots, lamps, and figures. One of the most important traditional mineral uses
  • Chert and flint: Used for tools and weapons
  • Jade: Used for tools and ornaments
  • Meteorite iron: Used for tools before the introduction of iron from European contact (the Cape York meteorite was a significant source)
  • Gemstones: Rubies, sapphires, and other gemstones were used for ornaments and trade

Early European Exploration (1700s-1800s)

  • Hans Egede (1721): Danish-Norwegian missionary who established a colony in Greenland. Noted mineral occurrences during his travels
  • Geological surveys: Danish geologists conducted geological mapping and mineral exploration throughout the 19th century
  • Cryolite mining (1854-1987): The Ivittuut cryolite mine in South Greenland was one of the world's most important cryolite deposits. Cryolite was essential for aluminum smelting until the process was changed. Ivittuut was Greenland's first industrial mine

20th Century Exploration

  • GEUS established (1946): The Geological Survey of Denmark and Greenland began systematic geological mapping and mineral exploration
  • Lead-zinc mining at Mestersvig (1956-1963): A small lead-zinc mine in East Greenland operated by the Danish government. Short-lived due to low grades and remote location
  • Coal mining at Qullissat (1924-1972): A coal mine on Disko Island in West Greenland. Produced coal for local consumption
  • Exploration boom (1980s-1990s): Rising commodity prices attracted international exploration companies to Greenland. Gold, zinc, lead, and base metal exploration programs were conducted across the territory

21st Century Mining

Nalunaq Gold Mine (2004-2013):

  • Greenland's first commercial gold mine, located in South Greenland
  • Operated by Nalunaq Gold Mine A/S (a Greenlandic-Danish joint venture)
  • Historical production: approximately 350,000 ounces of gold
  • Open-pit operation transitioning to underground
  • Closed in 2013 due to low gold prices and operational challenges
  • Currently on care and maintenance; potential for future restart under new ownership

Ruby Mining (2017-present):

  • LNS Greenland operates the Aappaluttoq ruby mine in South Greenland
  • Greenland's first colored gemstone mine
  • Produces high-quality rubies known for their unique color
  • Demonstrates that commercial mining can operate successfully in Greenland

Kvanefjeld Exploration:

  • Greenland Minerals (Chinese-backed) explored the Kvanefjeld rare earth-uranium-zinc deposit in South Greenland

Founded and led by CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML) controls the Skaergaard project in southeast Greenland.

> Note: Kvanefjeld is a rare earth-uranium project owned by Greenland Minerals Limited (ASX: GGG), a completely separate company from Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML).

  • Environmental permit was approved, but the project was suspended following Greenland's uranium policy changes
  • Remains one of the world's largest undeveloped REE deposits

The Skaergaard Story

  • 1930s: The Skaergaard intrusion is identified by Danish geologist L.R. Wager as a world-class example of a layered mafic intrusion
  • 1960s-1980s: Geological mapping and research establishes the scientific significance of Skaergaard. PGM and gold mineralization is noted but not systematically explored
  • 1990s-2000s: Commercial exploration begins, with diamond drilling defining PGM and gold mineralization
  • 2010s: Continued exploration and resource definition. The project changes hands between several companies
  • 2020s: GRML (formerly Klotho Neurosciences) acquires the project and rebrands to focus on Skaergaard development. Resource estimate of 25.4M oz PdEq + 23.5M oz AuEq published. Advancing toward feasibility and EIA

Lessons from Greenland's Mining History

Several lessons emerge from Greenland's mining history:

  • Remote location is the primary challenge: Every mining project in Greenland has faced significant logistical challenges
  • Commodity prices matter: Nalunaq, Qullissat, and Mestersvig all closed when commodity prices made operations uneconomic
  • Environmental standards have evolved: Modern environmental requirements are much more stringent than when earlier mines operated
  • First-mover complexity: Each mining project has faced unique challenges as the first of its kind in Greenland. Skaergaard will be no different
  • Danish governance provides stability: Greenland's mining history has been conducted within a consistent Danish legal framework, providing stability and predictability

Conclusion

Greenland's mining history spans from indigenous mineral use through Danish colonial-era mining to the modern era of commercial exploration and development. The Nalunaq gold mine and Aappaluttoq ruby mine demonstrate that commercial mining can operate in Greenland. The Skaergaard project represents the next chapter in this history, with the potential to be the largest and most significant mining development in Greenland's history. The lessons from past projects inform the approach to Skaergaard's development and provide confidence in the jurisdiction's mining potential.
