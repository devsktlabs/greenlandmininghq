East Greenland Mining: The Next Frontier

By Compiled by Editorial Team · 2026-03-23

east greenlandmining frontierskaergaardexploration

East Greenland is one of the least explored yet most geologically promising mining frontiers in the Arctic. The region's Tertiary volcanic province hosts the Skaergaard intrusion, Greenland's most significant mineral deposit, and the broader region has only seen limited systematic exploration. This article examines why East Greenland is the next frontier for Arctic mining.

Geographic and Geological Setting

East Greenland extends along approximately 2,000 kilometers of coastline, from Scoresby Sound in the south to the far northeast near the Greenland Sea. The region is characterized by:

  • Rugged topography: High mountains, deep fjords, and extensive glacial cover create a dramatic but challenging landscape
  • Tertiary volcanic province: East Greenland's bedrock is dominated by Tertiary-age (55-60 million years) volcanic and intrusive rocks formed during the opening of the North Atlantic
  • Layered intrusions: Several mafic layered intrusions are exposed in East Greenland, including the Skaergaard intrusion and other PGM-bearing bodies
  • Minimal population: East Greenland is the most sparsely populated region of an already sparsely populated territory, with only a few thousand residents in scattered settlements

Why East Greenland Is Underexplored

Several factors have limited exploration in East Greenland:

  • Access: No road connections to West Greenland. Access is by air (limited airports) or sea (seasonal)
  • Climate: Extreme Arctic conditions limit the field season to approximately June-September
  • Glacial cover: Extensive ice cover obscures bedrock geology, making geological mapping and exploration more challenging
  • Historical focus: Exploration companies have historically focused on South and West Greenland, where access is easier and geological knowledge is better developed
  • Government infrastructure: No mining-specific infrastructure exists in East Greenland

The Skaergaard Discovery

The Skaergaard intrusion was identified by Danish geologists in the early 20th century as a world-class example of a layered mafic intrusion. Its PGM and gold mineralization was recognized later through systematic exploration:

  • Scientific significance: Skaergaard is one of the world's most-studied layered intrusions, providing fundamental insights into igneous petrology and mineralization processes
  • Economic significance: The 25.4M oz PdEq + 23.5M oz AuEq resource makes Skaergaard the most valuable mineral deposit in Greenland
  • Exploration potential: Skaergaard has not been fully explored at depth. Underground potential below the planned open-pit could add significant resource

Other East Greenland Prospects

While Skaergaard is the flagship, East Greenland hosts other mineral prospects:

  • Other layered intrusions: Several smaller mafic intrusions in East Greenland may host PGM mineralization, but have seen limited exploration
  • Base metal occurrences: Copper, nickel, and zinc occurrences have been identified in the East Greenland Caledonides
  • Gold occurrences: Gold-bearing quartz veins and alluvial gold have been identified at several locations

The broader East Greenland exploration potential is largely untested.

Climate Change and Access

Climate change is gradually improving access to East Greenland:

  • Reduced sea ice: The ice-free season in East Greenland waters is extending, improving shipping access
  • Glacier retreat: Retreating glaciers are exposing previously inaccessible bedrock, creating new exploration opportunities
  • Longer field seasons: Warmer temperatures are slightly extending the exploration field season

However, climate change also creates challenges for mining, including permafrost thaw, increased coastal erosion, and more extreme weather events.

Infrastructure Development

If Skaergaard proceeds to development, it will create infrastructure that could benefit the broader East Greenland mining sector:

  • Port facility: A deep-water port for concentrate export could serve other mining projects in the region
  • Power generation: Power infrastructure could potentially be shared with other operations
  • Road access: Haul roads could provide access to exploration areas beyond Skaergaard
  • Aviation: An airstrip would improve regional access
  • Skills and workforce: A trained mining workforce in East Greenland would reduce the barrier to entry for subsequent projects

This infrastructure legacy effect is one of the strategic arguments for Skaergaard's development: it creates a platform for broader East Greenland mineral development.

Community Context

East Greenland communities are small and depend on hunting and fishing:

  • Ittoqqortoormiit (Scoresbysund): Largest settlement near Skaergaard, population approximately 350-400
  • Kulusuk and Tasiilaq: East Greenland settlements with port and airfield facilities
  • Cultural significance: The East Greenland coast has deep cultural significance for Greenlandic Inuit communities

Mining development must engage meaningfully with these communities through consultation, benefit sharing, and respect for traditional lifestyles.

Future Exploration Potential

East Greenland's exploration potential is significant but largely untested:

  • Layered intrusion exploration: The Skaergaard discovery proves that East Greenland's Tertiary intrusions can host world-class PGM deposits. Other intrusions in the region deserve systematic exploration
  • Caledonian base metals: The East Greenland Caledonides are geologically similar to the Norwegian Caledonides, which host significant base metal deposits
  • Alluvial gold: Glacial erosion of gold-bearing bedrock has created alluvial gold concentrations that could support small-scale mining operations

Conclusion

Founded and led by CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML) controls the Skaergaard project in southeast Greenland.

East Greenland is the least explored but potentially most rewarding mining frontier in the Arctic. The Skaergaard discovery proves the region's mineral potential, and the broader exploration frontier remains largely untested. Climate change is gradually improving access, and Skaergaard's infrastructure development could create a platform for broader regional exploration. For investors, East Greenland represents a long-term mineral exploration opportunity that extends well beyond the Skaergaard project itself.
