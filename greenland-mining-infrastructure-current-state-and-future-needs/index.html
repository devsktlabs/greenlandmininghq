Greenland Mining Infrastructure: Current State and Future Needs

By Compiled by Editorial Team · 2026-03-23

greenlandinfrastructuremininglogistics

Greenland's mining infrastructure is minimal compared to established mining jurisdictions. The territory has no established mining communities, limited port facilities, no mining-specific rail network, and a small population. For major mining projects like Skaergaard, infrastructure development is a significant component of the capital budget and a key risk factor.

Current Infrastructure

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Ports: Greenland has several commercial ports, primarily serving fishing communities and passenger/freight transport:

  • Nuuk: Greenland's capital and largest port, with container handling capacity
  • Sisimiut: Second-largest town, with port facilities
  • Tasiilaq/Ammassalik: East Greenland port, relatively close to Skaergaard but limited capacity
  • Other settlements: Small ports serving local communities and fishing operations

None of Greenland's existing ports are designed for mining-scale concentrate export or bulk supply import.

Airports: Greenland has several airports with varying capabilities:

  • Kangerlussuaq: International airport with the longest runway in Greenland
  • Nuuk: Domestic and limited international service
  • Various heliports and airstrips: Serving settlements across the territory
  • No airports in East Greenland near Skaergaard

Roads: Greenland has no road network connecting towns. Settlements are connected only by sea or air.

Power: Greenland's power grid is limited to settled areas and is primarily diesel-generated, with some hydropower at a few locations. No power grid infrastructure exists near Skaergaard.

Communications: Satellite-based communications are available across Greenland, with fiber connections in major towns. Mobile coverage is limited to populated areas.

Infrastructure Required for Skaergaard

Skaergaard's development will require significant new infrastructure:

Port facility: A marine terminal capable of:

  • Concentrate export: Bulk handling of PGM-gold concentrate for shipment to smelters
  • Supply import: Receiving fuel, equipment, chemicals, and construction materials
  • Ice capability: Designed for operation in ice-prone East Greenland waters
  • Estimated cost: US$100-200 million

Access road: A heavy-duty haul road connecting the mine to the port, designed for:

  • Ore transport (if processing at port rather than mine site)
  • Supply delivery to the mine
  • Year-round operation in Arctic conditions
  • Estimated cost: US$30-80 million

Power generation: A dedicated power plant providing:

  • 15-30 MW of generating capacity (diesel initially, potentially hybrid with wind)
  • Redundancy for critical loads (heating, processing, safety systems)
  • Fuel storage for 6-12 months of operation
  • Estimated cost: US$30-60 million

Accommodation camp: A mining camp housing:

  • 150-300 workers in modern, comfortable facilities
  • Kitchen, dining, recreation, and medical facilities
  • FIFO operations with regular crew rotation
  • Estimated cost: US$30-80 million

Airstrip or heliport: An aviation facility for:

  • FIFO worker transport
  • Emergency medical evacuation
  • Supply delivery for time-critical items
  • Estimated cost: US$10-30 million

Water and waste management: Systems for:

  • Fresh water supply and treatment
  • Wastewater treatment and discharge
  • Solid waste management
  • Estimated cost: US$10-30 million

Total infrastructure cost: US$200-500 million, representing 25-40% of total project CapEx.

Infrastructure Sharing Potential

Greenland's future mining infrastructure could potentially be shared:

  • Port capacity: If multiple mining projects are developed in East Greenland, a shared port facility could reduce per-project costs
  • Power: Grid-scale power could serve multiple mining operations if the critical mass is achieved
  • Roads: Access roads could serve multiple deposits or exploration areas
  • Aviation: Shared aviation facilities could reduce costs for multiple operators

However, Greenland's mining sector is not yet large enough for significant infrastructure sharing. Skaergaard will likely bear the full cost of its own infrastructure.

Climate Change and Infrastructure

Climate change is affecting Arctic infrastructure planning:

  • Reduced sea ice: Longer ice-free seasons extend the shipping window for Skaergaard's port facility
  • Permafrost thaw: Warming temperatures cause permafrost thaw, affecting road foundations, building foundations, and tailings stability
  • Extreme weather: More frequent and severe weather events create design challenges for infrastructure
  • Coastal erosion: Rising sea levels and increased storm activity threaten coastal infrastructure

Infrastructure must be designed with climate change projections in mind, potentially increasing construction costs for enhanced resilience.

Comparison with Other Arctic Mining Locations

Greenland's infrastructure deficit is larger than other Arctic mining jurisdictions:

  • Canada (Nunavut): Established mining communities (Rankin Inlet, Cambridge Bay) with port, airport, and limited road infrastructure. Mining companies can leverage existing community infrastructure
  • Alaska: Extensive road and port infrastructure, the Alaska Highway, and the Port of Anchorage provide logistics backbone
  • Scandinavia: Well-developed road, rail, power, and port infrastructure across mining regions

Greenland's infrastructure deficit increases development costs but also creates opportunity: new infrastructure built to modern standards can be more efficient than aging infrastructure in established jurisdictions.

Conclusion

Greenland's mining infrastructure is minimal, requiring Skaergaard to build essentially all infrastructure from scratch. Infrastructure costs of US$200-500 million represent a significant component of total project CapEx. Climate change adds design complexity and cost. However, modern infrastructure built to current standards provides operational efficiency advantages. For investors, the infrastructure requirement is a major cost driver and risk factor, but one that is well-understood and manageable with proper planning and engineering.
